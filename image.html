1. Dataset Exploration & Preprocessing

Dataset Overview: CIFAR10

Source: Hugging Face - CIFAR10 Dataset

The project utilizes the CIFAR10 (Canadian Institute For Advanced Research - 10) dataset, a standard benchmark for image classification tasks. The primary objective is to evaluate and compare the performance of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Vision Transformer (ViT) architectures.

60,000 (50,000 train + 10,000 test) Total Samples
10 Unique Classes
Visual Information (Image) Modalities

Data Characteristics:

  • Color Profile: Pixel intensity analysis shows a non-uniform distribution across channels, specifically a high frequency in the Blue channel (~70 intensity), likely due to sky or water backgrounds.
  • Visual Data: The img attribute consists of color images with a native resolution of 32×32 pixels, representing 10 distinct object categories.
  • Label Distribution: The dataset is perfectly balanced, with each of the 10 classes containing exactly 6,000 samples (5,000 for training and 1,000 for testing).

Full Class List (10 Classes):

Airplane, Automobile, Bird, Cat, Deer, Dog, Frog, Horse, Ship, Truck.

Class Distribution Analysis

What:

Examine the number of samples across all 10 categories to ensure a uniform distribution within the CIFAR-10 dataset for both training and testing sets.

Why:

Class imbalance can heavily bias the model's predictions toward majority classes (e.g., assuming everything is a "Frog" if frogs dominate the data). A perfectly balanced dataset ensures the model learns distinctive features for every class equally, preventing skewed accuracy metrics.

How:

Calculate the frequency of each class label, verify that each contains exactly 5,000 training and 1,000 testing samples, and visualize the results using a bar chart to confirm the lack of a "long-tail" distribution.

As we can see in Figure 1 and 2, the horizontal 'flat' line across all bars confirms that our dataset is perfectly balanced. This provides a solid foundation for fair evaluation across all organic and inorganic categories.

Statistical Color Profiling (Pixel Distribution)

What:

Analyze the raw pixel intensity distribution across the Red, Green, and Blue (RGB) channels to identify global color biases and brightness patterns within the CIFAR-10 images.

Why:

Color distributions reveal fundamental characteristics of the dataset environment, such as the dominance of outdoor backgrounds (sky/sea). Understanding this non-uniformity provides the scientific justification for applying per-channel Normalization to ensure the model focuses on structural features rather than lighting or color shifts.

How:

Aggregate the pixel values (0-255) for each channel from the entire dataset, compute their frequency, and visualize the results using a histogram. Calculate the specific mean and standard deviation for each channel to use as input parameters for the pre-processing normalization layer.

Our analysis of raw pixel intensities across RGB channels reveals a dominant Blue channel (~70 intensity), suggesting a high frequency of cool-toned backgrounds (sky, water). A sharp spike at 255 across all channels indicates saturated regions or bright highlights.

Co-occurrence Analysis

What:

Quantify the visual and structural relationships between different categories by calculating the Cosine Similarity between their raw 32 x 32 pixel vectors.

Why:

High similarity scores (near 1.0) indicate "Semantic Overlap," where different classes share nearly identical pixel patterns. Identifying these clusters—such as the Animal cluster (0.998 similarity) — allows us to predict and justify specific model failures (misclassifications) before the training even begins.

How:

Compute the mean pixel vector for each class, perform pairwise Cosine Similarity calculations to build a relationship matrix, and visualize the findings using a Heatmap. This statistical proximity provides the empirical evidence needed to justify using deep architectures like ResNet50 for complex feature separation.

The correlation matrix (or Similarity Heatmap) visualizes how certain classes share visual features. Significant overlap is observed in the Animal cluster (e.g., Cat often shares features with Dog) and the Vehicle cluster (Automobile vs. Truck).

Interactive Training Pipeline

Input (CIFAR10)

In this initial stage, the system retrieves and structures the raw visual information required for single-label image classification.

  • Single-label Encoding: Unlike multi-label datasets, each CIFAR-10 image belongs to exactly one of the 10 mutually exclusive categories. Labels are maintained as integer indices (0-9) which are later transformed into One-hot vectors (implicitly via CrossEntropyLoss) during the training phase.
  • Visual Input: Raw 32 x 32 images are streamed directly from the Hugging Face hub. These images are processed as PIL objects before being converted into numerical tensors. This provides the spatial data necessary for the ResNet50 and ViT backbones to capture low-level features like edges and textures.
  • Data Partitioning: The dataset is split into Training, Validation (10%), and Testing sets. This ensures that the model is optimized on seen data but evaluated on completely unseen samples to verify its generalization capability.

Pre-process (Normalization + Augmentation)

In this stage, raw images are transformed into optimized tensors to ensure numerical stability and increase the model's ability to generalize across unseen data.

  • Per-Channel Normalization: We apply a global mean and standard deviation (μ, σ) subtraction across the RGB channels. This centers the data around zero, preventing vanishing or exploding gradients and significantly accelerating model convergence.
  • Data Augmentation (Training Only): To prevent overfitting on the 45,000 training samples, we employ Random Horizontal Flips and Random Crops. This artificially expands the dataset by teaching the model that a "Cat" is still a "Cat" even if mirrored or slightly shifted.
  • Data Partitioning: The dataset is split into Training, Validation (10%), and Testing sets. This ensures that the model is optimized on seen data but evaluated on completely unseen samples to verify its generalization capability.
  • ResNet50 Stream (Native 32x32): Applied RandomCrop(32, padding=4) and RandomHorizontalFlip() for stochastic augmentation. Images are normalized using CIFAR-10 specific statistics (μ, σ) = (0.4914, 0.4822, 0.4465) to stabilize gradient flow.
  • ViT-Tiny Stream (Upscaled 224x224): Images are upsampled to 224x224 to align with the Transformer's patch embedding logic. We use ImageNet-standard normalization (μ, σ) = (0.485, 0.456, 0.406) to maintain compatibility with the pre-trained backbone.

Backbone & Classification Head)

We adopt a Transfer Learning strategy by utilizing state-of-the-art architectures pre-trained on the ImageNet-1K dataset. Each model consists of a feature-extracting Backbone and a specialized Classification Head.

ResNet50 (CNN)
  • Backbone: Uses 50 layers of residual blocks to extract local spatial features. It is highly efficient for 32 x 32 images due to its inherent Spatial Inductive Bias.
  • Head: A Global Average Pooling (GAP) layer followed by a single Linear Layer (2048 -> 10), mapping deep features to the 10 CIFAR-10 classes.
ViT-Tiny (Vision Transformer)
  • Backbone: Processes the image as a sequence of 16 x 16 patches. It captures Global Context through Multi-Head Self-Attention. We chose the Tiny variant to optimize performance within our 16GB RAM constraint.
  • Head: Uses the final state of the [CLS] token (dimension 192) fed into a Linear Layer (192 -> 10) to produce the final class logits.

4. Error Analysis

To gain a deeper understanding of the model's limitations, we performed both quantitative and qualitative error analyses on the validation set. We specifically focused on "Critical Failures," defined as samples where the Jaccard Similarity (the overlap between predicted and true genres) was below 0.2.

4.1 Quantitative Analysis: Critical Error Rate

Based on our evaluation of the validation set (approximately 1,520 samples):

  • Total Critical Errors (Jaccard < 0.2): 152 samples.
  • Critical Error Rate: ~10.0%.
Observation: While the model achieves a high overall Micro-F1 (0.66), 1 in 10 movies results in a near-total misclassification. This indicates that despite general accuracy, the model remains susceptible to strong "distractors" in either the textual or visual modality.

4.2 Qualitative Case Study: Keyword Bias & Visual Ambiguity

We analyzed a representative case of critical failure to understand the interplay between textual and visual features.

Analysis of Failure Modes:

  1. Keyword Bias (Textual Over-reliance): The plot includes phrases like "South Korean underworld" and "defeating an army of gangsters", which are strongly associated with Crime and Thriller genres in the training data. As a result, the model overemphasizes these keywords, failing to capture the comedic and romantic elements that define the film's true genre.
  2. Visual Ambiguity (Visual Misinterpretation): Action-comedy posters often feature high-contrast lighting and weaponry to attract attention. The Vision Transformer (ViT) interprets these cues similarly to those in serious Crime/Drama films, as it lacks the ability to recognize subtle indicators of comedy, such as exaggerated expressions or vibrant color tones.

4.3 The Overconfidence Problem

A striking finding was the model's high confidence in its errors. In the case above, the model predicted Drama with a confidence score of 92.62%, despite it being incorrect.

Model Over-calibration

Modern architectures trained with BCE Loss tend to be "over-confident." The loss function penalizes the model if it doesn't push probabilities toward 1 or 0, leading to aggressive maximization of a class if a single strong feature (like the word "gangster") is detected.

"Safe Bet" Strategy

Drama appears in nearly 50% of the MMIMDb dataset. The model learns that predicting "Drama" for any narrative with emotional weight is a statistically safe bet, leading to a systemic bias toward this label with high certainty.

4.4 Systemic Patterns from Quantitative Data

The following visualizations illustrate the distribution of errors across genres and the density of critical failures:

📊 Fig 4.4a: Analysis of Critical Prediction Errors by Genre

Comedy, Thriller, and Romance show the highest False Negatives. These genres rely on subtle emotional and narrative cues that the late-fusion approach struggles to capture.

📈 Fig 4.4b: Distribution of Prediction Overlap in Failed Samples

Many critical errors fall within the 0.0 - 0.1 Jaccard score range, representing complete prediction failures where one modality's misleading signals overpower the other's correct cues.

  • Tonal Blindness: Comedy, Thriller, and Romance show the highest False Negatives. Unlike genres such as "Action" or "Horror" with clear visual and textual signals, these rely on subtle emotional and narrative cues. The current late-fusion approach (DistilBERT + ViT concatenation) struggles to capture these nuances, often missing them in predictions.
  • The "Safe Bet" Bias: There is a significant spike in False Positives for the Drama category. Due to class imbalance—where Drama dominates the dataset—the model adopts a “safe bet” strategy, frequently defaulting to this label when inputs are ambiguous.
  • Modality Disconnect: Many critical errors fall within the 0.0 - 0.1 Jaccard score range, representing complete prediction failures. In these cases, misleading signals from one modality (e.g., strong keywords or visuals) overpower correct cues from the other, exposing the weakness of simple feature concatenation in handling cross-modal interactions.

8. Conclusion

In this assignment, we developed and evaluated multiple approaches for movie genre classification using the MMIMDb dataset, ranging from zero-shot multimodal heuristics to full end-to-end fine-tuning of Vision Transformer (ViT) and DistilBERT architectures.

Key Findings & Insights

Multimodal Superiority

The fully fine-tuned fusion model (Micro-F1: 0.6624) significantly outperformed the best Zero-shot baseline (Micro-F1: 0.3219).

This confirms that movie genres are often metaphorical and context-dependent, requiring the deep parameter updates of specialized Transformers to capture nuanced relationships that general-purpose alignments miss.

Efficiency of Few-Shot Learning

By training on only 5% of the data (622 samples), the Few-Shot Linear Probe achieved 89% of the performance of the full fine-tuned version (Micro-F1: 0.5908).

This demonstrates a highly cost-effective strategy for labeling niche cinematic databases where large-scale human annotation is not feasible, yet high precision is required.
